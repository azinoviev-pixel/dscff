The Hidden Chain: Why Psychiatric Symptoms Persist Despite IV Iron

Cholecystectomy → Vitamin D Malabsorption → Hepcidin → Brain Iron Deficit → Neurotransmitter Failure
Patient: Veronika Pogrebova, 33 y.o. | Cholecystectomy: 2021 | FQAD: since Feb 2024
Current Vitamin D: 25 ng/mL (deficient, stuck for months despite supplementation)
Serum iron: 154 µg/dL | TSAT: 52.6% | CRP: <0.03 (no inflammation)
Ferritin: not measured (pending) | Estradiol: not measured (pending)
1

No Gallbladder → Fat-Soluble Vitamins Not Absorbed

Without gallbladder, no concentrated bile bolus at mealtimes → impaired micelle formation → vitamin D (and CoQ10, Omega-3, K2) pass through GI tract unabsorbed. Post-cholecystectomy bile salt pool shrinks to ~50% of normal; postprandial bile concentration stays at basal levels instead of spiking.

Polat & Beyazal, Arch Osteoporosis 2018: post-cholecystectomy women had 15–22% lower 25(OH)D (p=0.03). Pomare & Heaton, Gut 1973: bile salt pool reduced by half. Maislos & Shany 1987: vitamin D absorption almost completely abolished without bile (p<0.001).

↓

Vitamin D Stuck at 25 ng/mL → Hepcidin Not Suppressed

Vitamin D directly suppresses hepcidin transcription via a vitamin D response element (VDRE) on the HAMP gene promoter. At 25 ng/mL, this suppression is inadequate. A single vitamin D bolus produced a 73% decrease in hepcidin within one week. With CRP <0.03 (no inflammation), low vitamin D may be the primary remaining driver of elevated hepcidin.

Bacchetta et al., JASN 2013: identified VDRE on HAMP gene — vitamin D directly suppresses hepcidin independent of inflammation. Smith et al. 2017: single vitamin D bolus → 73% hepcidin decrease in 7 days.

↓

Elevated Hepcidin → Iron Trapped in Macrophages

Hepcidin binds to ferroportin (the only cellular iron exporter) and triggers its internalization and degradation. Iron is physically locked inside macrophages and enterocytes — present in the body but unavailable for circulation. Even after 4 IV iron infusions, hepcidin can recapture iron from plasma back into macrophage storage.

Ganz & Nemeth, Blood 2012: hepcidin-ferroportin axis as master regulator of systemic iron homeostasis. Nemeth et al., Science 2004: hepcidin causes ferroportin internalization within hours.

↓

Brain Iron Deficit Persists Despite Good Peripheral Numbers

Iron crosses the blood-brain barrier (BBB) only via holo-transferrin binding to TfR1 receptors on brain endothelium. TfR1 has 500× higher affinity for iron-loaded transferrin than empty transferrin. If hepcidin continuously recaptures circulating iron, transferrin saturation drops over time → brain sees empty transporters → iron delivery fails. Brain is last in line and takes 3–6 months to replete even after peripheral normalization.

Simpson et al., J Cereb Blood Flow Metab 2015: transferrin does not cross BBB — iron is released in endosomes and transported separately. Earley et al., Neurology 2000: CSF ferritin 65% lower in iron-deficient patients even with normal peripheral markers.

↓

TPH2 and TH Remain Substrate-Limited → No Serotonin, No Dopamine

Tryptophan hydroxylase 2 (TPH2, serotonin synthesis) and tyrosine hydroxylase (TH, dopamine synthesis) both require Fe²⁺ as an obligate cofactor in their catalytic center. Without brain iron, these rate-limiting enzymes cannot function regardless of substrate availability.

Beard et al., J Nutr 2003 (PMID 12612143): 65% reduction in midbrain serotonin synthesis in iron-deficient rats. Lozoff et al. 2006: iron deficiency reduces striatal D2 receptor density and TH activity.

↓

DOUBLE HIT: Vitamin D Also Directly Activates Serotonin Synthesis

Independent of iron, vitamin D activates TPH2 transcription via a VDRE on the TPH2 gene. At 25 ng/mL, this transcriptional activation is suboptimal. Active vitamin D (1,25(OH)₂D) increased TPH2 expression 28–33 fold, repressed serotonin reuptake transporter (SERT) 51–59% (mimicking an SSRI), repressed MAO-A 51–59%, and enhanced serotonin concentrations 2.9-fold. The patient's serotonin system is hit from both directions: no iron cofactor AND no vitamin D transcriptional activation.

Patrick & Ames, FASEB J 2015: vitamin D activates TPH2 and represses TPH1 via VDREs. Sabir et al. 2018: quantified TPH2 ↑28–33×, SERT ↓51–59%, MAO-A ↓51–59%, serotonin ↑2.9× in serotonergic neurons treated with 1,25(OH)₂D.

↓

Result: Psychiatric Symptoms Persist Despite IV Iron and Good Peripheral Labs

Serum iron 154 µg/dL and TSAT 52.6% look excellent on paper. But if hepcidin remains elevated (due to vitamin D at 25 instead of 40–60 ng/mL), iron is being continuously recaptured, brain iron lags months behind peripheral levels, and serotonin/dopamine synthesis remains impaired. SSRIs are futile — there is no serotonin to block reuptake of.

Breaking the Chain — Three Interventions

1. Ox Bile 250 mg with every fat-containing meal → restores micelle formation → all fat-soluble supplements (Vitamin D, CoQ10, Omega-3, K2) begin absorbing properly.

2. Vitamin D buccal spray 4,000–5,000 IU daily → bypasses GI tract entirely → 2.6× more effective than capsules in malabsorption (Satia et al. 2015). Target: 40–60 ng/mL within 8 weeks.

3. Magnesium glycinate 400 mg elemental/day → cofactor for CYP27B1 (converts calcifediol to active calcitriol in mitochondria). Without magnesium, even absorbed vitamin D cannot be activated. Fluoroquinolones chelate and deplete magnesium stores.

Expected result: Vitamin D rises to 40–60 ng/mL → hepcidin suppressed by up to 73% → ferroportin preserved → iron stays in circulation → brain iron delivery normalizes over 3–6 months → TPH2 and TH regain both cofactor (iron) and transcriptional activation (vitamin D) → serotonin and dopamine synthesis resumes.
